Fleet Management

Deploying a fleet is a project. Managing a fleet is a practice. The provisioning pipelines from Chapter 11 get readers online and configured, but the moment they enter production, a different set of problems takes over. Firmware ages and accumulates vulnerabilities. TLS certificates march toward expiration on a fixed calendar, indifferent to your release schedule. Wi-Fi credentials rotate on the network team's timeline, not yours. Batteries degrade. Readers develop patterns of failure that are invisible at the individual-device level but obvious when you look at the fleet as a whole.

This chapter covers the four operational disciplines that determine whether a fleet deployment survives its first year: firmware lifecycle management, cryptographic credential rotation, network credential rotation, and fleet-wide health observability. Each discipline addresses a specific failure mode, and the patterns in this chapter exist to prevent those failures from cascading into fleet-wide outages.

12.1 Firmware Updates at Scale​

Firmware updates to physical hardware carry fundamentally different risk than deploying a new version of a microservice. When you push a container update to a Kubernetes cluster and it fails, you roll back in seconds; the previous image is cached, the orchestrator handles the swap, and your users experience a brief interruption at most. When you push a firmware update to an RFID reader bolted to a dock door in a distribution center 600 miles away and it fails, the reader may be left in a degraded or unbootable state. There is no cached previous image. There is no orchestrator. There is a truck roll.

This asymmetry demands a different deployment methodology; one built on blast-radius control, mandatory pre-flight validation, and post-update verification.

The set_os Command​

The set_os command initiates a firmware update by providing a URL from which the reader downloads the firmware package. After a successful download and integrity check, the reader automatically reboots to apply the update.

{
  "command": "set_os",
  "requestId": "fw-update-001",
  "firmwareUpdate": {
    "url": "https://firmware.enterprise.com/rfid/rfd40-3.11.0.zip",
    "version": "3.11.0"
  }
}

Publish to the reader's MGMT command topic:

enterprise/MGMT/clients/cmnd/RFD40-212735201D0053

The version field is optional but strongly recommended; it serves as a declarative record of the expected post-update firmware version, which your automation uses for verification in the final stage.

Response codes that matter for automation:

CodeMeaningAutomation Action
0Download started, reboot imminentWait for reconnection, then verify
1Update queuedMonitor for completion
19Download failedCheck URL accessibility from the reader's network
20Validation failedFirmware package may be corrupted; verify checksum
21Insufficient batteryAbort; do not retry until battery is charged
22Operation in progressStop the active operation first, then retry

⚠ WARNING: Never push set_os to the entire fleet simultaneously. A firmware bug that passes lab testing but fails under production load will affect every reader at once. Staged rollouts (described below) limit the blast radius to a small, recoverable cohort.

Pre-Flight Checks​

Before issuing set_os to any reader, your automation must verify three conditions. Skipping any of these risks bricking the device or corrupting its state.

1. Battery level ≥ 30%. A firmware update involves downloading a package over Wi-Fi, writing it to flash storage, and rebooting. If the battery dies mid-write, the firmware image may be corrupted and the reader may not boot. The set_os command returns response code 21 (Insufficient battery) if the reader's internal check fails, but your automation should enforce a higher threshold than the device's own minimum to provide margin.

Check battery level via the most recent heartbeat's batteryStatus.chargePercentage or by issuing get_status:

{
  "command": "get_status",
  "requestId": "preflight-battery-001"
}

If chargePercentage is below 30%, skip this reader and schedule the update for the next charging cycle.

2. No active RFID operation. Interrupting an inventory or access operation mid-cycle can leave tag state inconsistent, particularly for write or lock operations where the Gen2 protocol is mid-transaction. Check radioActivity in the heartbeat or get_status response. If "ACTIVE", either wait for the operation to complete or send control_operation STOP before proceeding.

3. Reader is connected and responsive. Verify the reader is online by confirming a recent heartbeat (within the expected interval) or by issuing any lightweight command (get_version, get_status) and receiving a response. Do not send set_os to a reader whose last heartbeat is stale; it may be offline, and the command will queue at the broker indefinitely.

Pre-flight summary:

CheckSourcePass CriteriaFail Action
BatteryHeartbeat or get_statuschargePercentage ≥ 30Skip; schedule for next charge cycle
Radio activityHeartbeat or get_statusradioActivity = "INACTIVE"Wait or send control_operation STOP
ConnectivityLast heartbeat timestampWithin 2× heartbeat intervalSkip; mark as unreachable

Staged Rollouts and Canary Deployments​

A staged rollout applies the update to progressively larger cohorts, with a soak period and health verification at each stage. If a problem emerges, it affects only the current cohort, not the entire fleet.

Recommended stages:

StageFleet %Cohort Size (500-reader fleet)Soak PeriodPurpose
Canary1%5 readers24 hoursDetect catastrophic failures (boot loops, connectivity loss)
Early adopter5%25 readers48 hoursDetect performance regressions, edge-case exceptions
Broad rollout25%125 readers24 hoursValidate at moderate scale, confirm no load-dependent issues
Full fleet100%345 remainingN/AComplete the rollout

Canary selection criteria. Choose canary readers that represent your fleet's diversity: at least one from each hardware model (RFD40, RFD90), at least one from each deployment site, and preferably readers with above-average exception rates (they are most likely to expose firmware regressions). Do not select your most reliable, least-used readers as canaries; they will pass any test and tell you nothing.

Soak period health criteria. During the soak period, monitor the updated cohort for:

SignalSourceFailure Indicator
Heartbeat continuityheartBeatEVTHeartbeat stops (reader crashed or entered boot loop)
Exception rateexceptionEVTRate increase compared to pre-update baseline
Tag read performancedataEVT volumeSignificant drop in read rate or total tags
Connection stabilitymqttConnEVTIncreased disconnection/reconnection frequency

If any signal degrades beyond acceptable thresholds during the soak period, halt the rollout. Investigate whether the new firmware introduced a regression. Do not proceed to the next stage until the canary cohort is stable.

Post-Update Verification​

After the reader reboots (30–60 seconds), verify the firmware update succeeded by issuing get_version:

{
  "command": "get_version",
  "requestId": "fw-verify-001"
}

Expected response:

{
  "command": "get_version",
  "requestId": "fw-verify-001",
  "apiVersion": "V1.1",
  "deviceVersion": {
    "model": "RFD40",
    "deviceSerialNo": "RFD40-212735201D0053",
    "sku": "RFD40-STANDARD-G",
    "firmwareVersion": "3.11.0",
    "bluetoothVersion": "1.2.45",
    "radioVersion": "2.1.57.0",
    "osVersion": "11.0.4.1",
    "bootLoaderVersion": "1.0.3",
    "cloudAgentVersion": "0.6.3.01"
  },
  "response": {
    "code": 0,
    "description": "Success"
  }
}

Compare deviceVersion.firmwareVersion against the expected version from the set_os payload. If they match, the update succeeded. If they do not match, or if the reader does not respond within 90 seconds of the set_os acknowledgment:

  1. Wait an additional 60 seconds, some updates take longer to apply on first boot.
  2. Retry get_version up to 3 times with exponential backoff.
  3. If the reader still does not respond or reports the old firmware version, flag it as a failed update requiring manual investigation.

Build a firmware version matrix. After a fleet-wide rollout, query get_version from every reader and build a version matrix:

Firmware VersionReader Count% of FleetStatus
3.11.048597%Current
3.10.27122.4%Previous; update pending
3.10.2730.6%Previous; update failed

Readers stuck on the old version after a completed rollout represent either pre-flight check failures (battery too low, offline at rollout time) or update failures requiring investigation. Track them explicitly; stragglers accumulate and become a security risk if left unaddressed.

12.2 Certificate Lifecycle Management​

TLS certificates expire. This is not a possibility; it is a certainty baked into the certificate's metadata at the moment it was issued. A certificate with a validTo date of 2027-01-01T00:00:00Z will stop working on that date regardless of how well the rest of your infrastructure is performing. When the MQTT broker's CA certificate expires and the readers still trust only the old CA, every TLS handshake fails. Every reader disconnects. The fleet goes dark.

Certificate expiration is the most common cause of fleet-wide outages in IoT deployments, and it is entirely preventable with a rotation practice.

Monitoring Certificate Expiration​

The get_installed_certificate command retrieves all certificates installed on a reader, including their validity dates:

{
  "command": "get_installed_certificate",
  "requestId": "cert-audit-001"
}

Response:

{
  "command": "get_installed_certificate",
  "requestId": "cert-audit-001",
  "apiVersion": "V1.1",
  "certificates": [
    {
      "name": "mqtt-broker-ca",
      "type": "mqtt",
      "issuer": "CN=Zebra Enterprise CA, O=Zebra Technologies",
      "subject": "CN=mqtt-broker.zebra.com",
      "validFrom": "2025-01-01T00:00:00Z",
      "validTo": "2027-01-01T00:00:00Z",
      "serialNumber": "0A:1B:2C:3D:4E:5F",
      "fingerprint": "A1:B2:C3:D4:E5:F6:01:23:..."
    },
    {
      "name": "reader-client-cert",
      "type": "client",
      "issuer": "CN=Zebra Enterprise CA, O=Zebra Technologies",
      "subject": "CN=RFD40-212735201D0053, O=Zebra Technologies",
      "validFrom": "2025-06-01T00:00:00Z",
      "validTo": "2026-06-01T00:00:00Z",
      "serialNumber": "1A:2B:3C:4D:5E:6F",
      "fingerprint": "B2:C3:D4:E5:F6:01:23:..."
    }
  ],
  "response": {
    "code": 0,
    "description": "Success"
  }
}

Build a fleet-wide expiration report. Run get_installed_certificate against every reader on a regular schedule (weekly is sufficient for most deployments) and extract the validTo field from each certificate. Aggregate the results into an expiration timeline:

Certificate NameTypeEarliest Expiry (Fleet)Readers AffectedDays Remaining
reader-client-certclient2026-06-0150053
mqtt-broker-camqtt2027-01-01500267

Alert thresholds:

Days Until ExpirySeverityAction
90INFOPlan rotation; issue new certificate from CA
30WARNINGBegin rotation; push new certificate to fleet
7CRITICALEmergency rotation; expedite at all costs
0OUTAGECertificates expired; fleet connectivity lost

⚠ WARNING: If you allow a certificate to expire without a rotation plan in place, every reader using that certificate loses its TLS connection simultaneously. Recovery requires pushing new certificates to every reader, which is impossible over MQTT if the TLS connection is already broken. Physical access or MDM intervention is required. Treat the 30-day warning threshold as a hard deadline.

Zero-Downtime Certificate Rotation​

The rotation pattern ensures that at no point during the process do readers lose connectivity. The key principle is overlap; the reader must hold both the old and new certificates during the transition window, and the broker must accept both during the same period.

Step 1: Issue the new certificate. Generate the replacement certificate from your CA with a validFrom date that overlaps with the current certificate's validTo. The new certificate should be valid starting now (or earlier), not starting on the day the old one expires.

Step 2: Install the new certificate on readers. Push the new certificate using install_certificate with a distinct name:

{
  "command": "install_certificate",
  "requestId": "cert-rotate-001",
  "certificate": {
    "name": "mqtt-broker-ca-2027",
    "type": "mqtt",
    "certificateData": "-----BEGIN CERTIFICATE-----\nMIIDxTCCAq2gAwIBAgI...\n-----END CERTIFICATE-----"
  }
}

Use a name that includes the year or version (mqtt-broker-ca-2027) to distinguish it from the current certificate. Verify installation on each reader with get_installed_certificate; the response should now list both the old and new certificates.

Step 3: Update the broker to present the new certificate. If you are rotating the broker's server certificate (the certificate the broker presents during TLS handshake), update the broker configuration to use the new certificate signed by the new CA. The broker should be configured to accept both the old and new CA during the overlap window.

If you are rotating the reader's client certificate (for mutual TLS), update the broker's trust store to accept the new client certificate in addition to the old one.

Step 4: Verify connectivity. After the broker is serving the new certificate and the readers have the new CA installed, verify that TLS handshakes succeed. Monitor mqttConnEVT events, any DISCONNECTED events with KEEPALIVE_TIMEOUT or BROKER_UNAVAILABLE reasons during this window may indicate a certificate mismatch.

Issue get_endpoint_config to confirm the MGMT endpoint's connectionStatus is "CONNECTED":

{
  "command": "get_endpoint_config",
  "requestId": "cert-verify-001"
}

If connectionStatus is "CONNECTED", the reader is successfully negotiating TLS with either the old or new certificate chain.

Step 5: Remove the old certificate (after expiry). Once the old certificate's validTo date has passed, clean up resources by removing it from the reader:

{
  "command": "delete_certificate",
  "requestId": "cert-cleanup-001",
  "certificate": {
    "name": "mqtt-broker-ca-2026"
  }
}

This step is housekeeping; the old certificate is already non-functional. But leaving expired certificates on devices clutters the certificate store and complicates future audits.

Rotation timeline visualization:

Old cert:  ╠══════════════════════════╗
           2025-01-01               2027-01-01 (expires)
                                    
New cert:         ╠══════════════════════════════════════╗
                  2026-10-01                            2029-01-01

             ←─── Overlap window ───→
            (both certs valid on reader)

The overlap window is the safety margin. As long as both certificates are installed on the reader and both are accepted by the broker, the TLS connection succeeds regardless of which certificate is actively used in the handshake. The longer the overlap, the more time you have to complete the fleet-wide rotation.

12.3 Wi-Fi Credential Rotation​

Wi-Fi credential rotation combines the connectivity risk from Chapter 11, Section 11.1 with the urgency of a security operation. The enterprise network team rotates the Wi-Fi pre-shared key or changes an 802.1X authentication credential. Every reader that does not receive the new credential before the old one is revoked loses network connectivity, and with it, MQTT reachability.

The challenge is not the set_wifi command itself. The challenge is coordination: the network infrastructure must accept the new credentials before you push them to readers, and the readers must be updated before the old credentials are revoked.

The Timing Problem​

Timeline:
                        ┌── Vulnerability window ──┐
Old creds active on AP: ╠══════════════════════════╣
New creds active on AP:              ╠══════════════════════════════╣
Reader credential push:                   ▲
                                     (Push here, both creds work)
Old creds revoked on AP:                            ▲
                                                (Revoke after fleet is updated)

The safe window for pushing new credentials to readers is after the new credentials are active on the access points but before the old credentials are revoked. If you push before the APs accept the new credentials, readers that apply the update immediately will disconnect and cannot reconnect. If you push after the old credentials are revoked, readers that haven't been updated yet disconnect.

⚠ WARNING: Do not push Wi-Fi credentials to readers until you have confirmed the new credentials are active and valid on the access point infrastructure. Pushing credentials that the APs do not yet accept disconnects the reader immediately. Recovery requires physical access or MDM intervention; the reader has no MQTT channel without network connectivity.

The Safe Rotation Sequence​

Step 1: Activate new credentials on the access point infrastructure. Coordinate with your network team to configure the new PSK or 802.1X credentials on all access points. The APs must accept both the old and new credentials during the transition window. Most enterprise wireless controllers (Cisco WLC, Aruba ClearPass, Zebra NX) support dual-PSK or overlapping RADIUS credentials for this purpose.

Step 2: Validate AP readiness. Before touching any reader, test the new credentials manually; connect a test device (laptop, phone, or a test reader on your bench) to the network using the new credentials. Confirm it authenticates and obtains an IP address. This step costs 60 seconds and prevents a fleet-wide outage.

Step 3: Push to a small canary cohort. Select 2–5 readers and send set_wifi with MODIFY:

{
  "command": "set_wifi",
  "requestId": "wifi-rotate-001",
  "wifiConfig": {
    "operation": "MODIFY",
    "ssid": "Warehouse-5G",
    "securityType": "WPA2_PERSONAL",
    "password": "new-warehouse-pass-2026-Q2"
  }
}

Monitor the canary readers for 5–10 minutes. Verify they remain online by checking:

  • Heartbeat events continue on the expected interval
  • get_wifi shows the correct SSID with "connected": true
  • mqttConnEVT does not show unexpected disconnections

If any canary reader disconnects and does not reconnect within 60 seconds, halt the rollout immediately. The new credentials may be incorrect or the APs may not have propagated the configuration change.

Step 4: Roll out to the full fleet. Push set_wifi MODIFY to the remaining readers in batches. Use the same staged rollout percentages as firmware updates (5% → 25% → 100%), but with shorter soak periods; Wi-Fi credential changes take effect immediately (no reboot required) and the success/failure signal is rapid (reconnection within 10–30 seconds).

Step 5: Revoke old credentials. Once all readers are confirmed online with the new credentials (verify via get_wifi across the fleet), coordinate with the network team to revoke the old credentials from the access points. This closes the transition window.

Enterprise Authentication (802.1X) Considerations​

For WPA2/WPA3-Enterprise deployments using EAP-TLS, credential rotation is actually a certificate rotation; the reader authenticates with a client certificate, not a password. This simplifies the reader-side operation (install a new wifi-type certificate via install_certificate) but adds a RADIUS server coordination step. Before pushing new client certificates to readers, ensure the RADIUS server's trust store accepts the new certificate's CA chain.

For PEAP or TTLS deployments, the credential is a username/password pair. Push via set_wifi MODIFY with the new identity and password values. The same timing coordination applies; the RADIUS server must accept the new credentials before readers attempt to use them.

Cross-reference: For the staged provisioning safety pattern and the connectivity trap, see Chapter 11, Section 11.1. For Wi-Fi EAP-TLS certificate installation, see Chapter 7.

12.4 Fleet Health Monitoring Patterns​

Chapter 10 explains what each event type means and how to handle it at the individual-device level. This section is about the view from 30,000 feet; the patterns that emerge when you aggregate event data across hundreds or thousands of readers and look for fleet-wide trends rather than individual anomalies.

The difference matters. A single reader going offline is a device problem. Fifteen readers going offline at the same site within the same 10-minute window is an infrastructure problem. A single RADIO_COMMUNICATION_ERROR exception is noise. A 3× increase in that exception type across all readers running firmware 3.11.0 is a firmware regression. Individual events are diagnostic. Fleet patterns are strategic.

Pattern 1: Heartbeat-Based Fleet Status Map​

The heartbeat event is the foundational fleet observability signal. Every reader that is configured to publish heartbeats (via config_events) sends a periodic pulse on:

{tenantId}/MGMT/clients/event/{deviceSerial}

Your fleet monitoring service subscribes to +/MGMT/clients/event/+ and tracks the last heartbeat timestamp for every reader in the fleet. From this single data point, you derive three states:

StateDefinitionDerivation
OnlineReader is operating normallyHeartbeat received within 1× the configured interval
StaleReader may be experiencing issuesLast heartbeat between 1× and 3× the configured interval
OfflineReader is unreachableNo heartbeat received for > 3× the configured interval

Why "stale" matters. A binary online/offline classification generates noise. Readers in large warehouses occasionally miss a single heartbeat due to Wi-Fi roaming, broker processing delays, or momentary network congestion. Treating a single missed heartbeat as an outage produces false alerts. The "stale" state absorbs this jitter; it signals "this reader has not reported recently, but it hasn't been gone long enough to confirm an outage." Only sustained absence (3× interval) triggers the offline classification and its associated alert.

Fleet status dashboard:

MetricCalculationOperational Question
Online countReaders with heartbeat age < 1× intervalHow many readers are currently operational?
Stale countReaders with heartbeat age between 1× and 3× intervalHow many readers may be developing connectivity issues?
Offline countReaders with heartbeat age > 3× intervalHow many readers need immediate investigation?
Online percentage(Online / Total) × 100What is the fleet's overall availability?

Operational target: A healthy fleet maintains > 98% online rate during operating hours. If the online percentage drops below 95%, investigate; it likely indicates an infrastructure issue (AP failure, broker overload, network partition) rather than individual device failures.

Data pipeline:

heartBeatEVT → MQTT subscriber → Last-seen timestamp table → 
  Scheduled evaluator (every 60s) → State classification → 
    Dashboard (fleet status map) + Alerting (offline count threshold)

Pattern 2: Exception Rate Trending​

Individual exception events are diagnostic tools; they tell you what went wrong on a specific reader at a specific time (Chapter 10, Section 10.3). Fleet-level exception trending tells you whether something is systematically wrong across a device population.

Ingest and aggregate. Subscribe to exception events on +/MGMT/clients/event/+, filter for "event": "exceptionEVT", and write each event to a time-series database with the following dimensions:

DimensionSource FieldPurpose
deviceSerialdeviceSerialPer-device trending
exceptionTypeexception.exceptionTypeError category
componentexception.componentSubsystem (RFID_RADIO, NETWORK, etc.)
firmwareVersionFleet inventory (from get_version)Firmware cohort correlation
siteFleet metadata (your provisioning database)Site-level correlation

Three views that reveal systemic issues:

View 1: Exception rate by firmware version. Plot exception count per hour, grouped by the firmware version running on the reporting device. If firmware 3.11.0 devices report 5× more RADIO_COMMUNICATION_ERROR exceptions than firmware 3.10.27 devices, the new firmware likely introduced a radio driver regression. This is the signal to halt the firmware rollout (Section 12.1) and investigate.

Exception rate by firmware version:

View 2: Exception rate by site. Plot exception count per hour, grouped by deployment site. A rising exception rate at a single site, while other sites remain stable, signals an environmental problem: Wi-Fi interference from new equipment, temperature issues from a broken HVAC system, or a failing access point creating intermittent NETWORK_ERROR exceptions.

View 3: Exception rate by device. Plot exception count per device over a rolling 7-day window. Devices with sustained exception rates above 5 per hour (the threshold suggested in Chapter 10, Section 10.3) are candidates for automated recovery (reboot) or manual investigation. A device with a rising trend toward that threshold, even if it has not yet crossed it, is a maintenance candidate.

Pattern 3: Battery Charge Fleet Distribution​

Battery degradation is a slow-motion fleet health problem. Individual readers send BATTERY_LOW and BATTERY_CRITICAL alerts when thresholds are crossed (Chapter 10, Section 10.2), but by the time a critical alert fires, the reader is minutes from shutting down. Fleet-level battery monitoring answers a different question: how many readers will need charging or battery replacement over the next shift?

Data source. If heartbeats are configured with batteryStatus: true (via config_events), every heartbeat includes chargePercentage. Collect this value from every reader on every heartbeat and store the latest reading per device.

Fleet battery distribution histogram. Bucket readers by charge level and visualize the distribution:

Charge RangeReader Count% of FleetOperational Interpretation
81–100%31262.4%Healthy, no action needed
61–80%9819.6%Normal; will need charging within 4–6 hours
41–60%5210.4%Monitor; schedule charging in next break window
21–40%285.6%Warning; charge soon or risk mid-shift dropout
0–20%102.0%Critical; charge immediately or readers will go offline

This histogram, updated every heartbeat interval, gives shift supervisors a proactive view of fleet readiness. A shift that starts with 15% of readers below 40% charge is a shift that will lose readers mid-operation unless docking stations are loaded.

Battery health trending. Beyond charge level, track stateOfHealth from the heartbeat's batteryStatus object (or from get_status). Aggregate by health category:

State of HealthReader CountAction
GOOD450No action
DEGRADED40Schedule battery replacement within 30 days
REPLACE10Replace battery immediately; capacity severely reduced

A reader with DEGRADED battery health charges fully but drains faster than peers. Over weeks, its chargePercentage curve slopes more steeply than the fleet average. Tracking this per-device trend over time, using heartbeat chargePercentage values collected into a time-series database, identifies replacement candidates before devices start generating BATTERY_LOW alerts during shifts.

Data pipeline:

heartBeatEVT (batteryStatus) → MQTT subscriber → Time-series DB →
  Latest-value snapshot (per device) → 
    Histogram dashboard (charge distribution) +
    Health table (stateOfHealth count) +
    Trend chart (per-device drain rate over 7 days) +
    Alert rule (> N devices below 20% charge)
